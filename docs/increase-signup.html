Increase sign ups

H&M, 2020

The challenge

15% of H&M online customers are not members of the loyalty program. How might we encourage them to sign up?

My role

Product Designer in a new Team in charge of increasing the number of sign ups.

The process

We were bombarded with requests to add more features, or put the sign up button in more places. But there was little data to back that up! So, I started by conducting research to inform our decisions.

Research

Main Research Questions

  • Are there issues stopping users from signing up?
  • Can users find where to sign up? Are we promoting sign up right?
  • Are there market specificities we should be aware of?

Methodologies used

  • Click tests on Userzoom.
  • Usability testing.
  • Previous in-house research into local experiences.
  • Quantitative data from Google Analytics and Clicktale.

Key takeaways

  • No need to go too heavy on the Sign ups CTAs. Users can find it already, and it's getting a bit too much.
  • We are not always promoting sign up in the right context, and users sometimes find the copy uninspiring.
  • Drop out rates on mobile (which accounts for 70% of H&M's visitors) is 30% higher than on desktop: no wonder, when it takes 40s longer to sign up, and there's severe usability issues (especially on Android).
  • In some countries, we must provide more sign-up options.

Prioritise

Based on those insights, the team and I established our priority. Then, I did a quick showcase of the research at our Sprint Demo to help our stakeholders understand the decisions.

Reduce friction

The problem

The date of birth was by far the biggest drop off point in the sign up form.

How might we solve it?

  • Stop to require the date of birth: The Product Owner and I started discussions with our stakeholders: Why do we need the date of birth? How else can we collect it? I prototyped a few ideas to help kickstart things.
  • Change the input method: with a precise date so far back in the past, a calendar (Android) or a scrollable list (iOS) are not the most appropriate. I reviewed existing research and guidelines (like Research by gov.uk and Material Design) and sketched out two ideas.

Design review

I took the ideas to our daily Design Review. We decided on the solution that allowed to stick to existing components in our Design System: simple input, split in 3 fields (day, month, year), with one visible label.

Results

Drop outs and time to complete the form decreased. And the rate of errors when filling in the form did not significantly change like we could have feared.

The next things to look at to reduce friction? Unclear password rules and error messages.

Focus on checkout

Opportunity

Promoting sign up in the checkout is where it makes the most sense for users: they get immediate benefits from it (like free shipping and discounts). And user testing pointed to some easy improvements, like tweaking the copy.

Ideation Workshop

I invited business experts, designers, product owners and analysts from Loyalty and Checkout teams to a workshop to generate ideas together. The workshop was very appreciated, as it brought together people who were not often in the same room, or didn’t feel involved in design decisions.

Outcomes

We generated a prioritised list of ideas to A/B test. The first one was to change the labels on the CTAs to continue to checkout, and it performed well.

Locally adapted

The problem

In the winter of 2020, the membership will be launched in a country where email usage is very low, and social sign up is preferred (I won't reveal details or show designs as the launch is still confidential).

Understand the market and the platform

I did some reading to understand the market better. And we met with our colleagues in that market to share knowledge and build a closer relationship.

Discuss technical limitations and draw up user flows

I worked closely with our IT Architect and Developers to understand technical limitations and draw up user flows where all scenarios are accounted for. I had to make assumptions about what would be the most user-friendly solutions, but was hoping to be able to test those soon.

Sketch, Design Review, Prototype

I sketched out different ideas for a layout to accommodate extra sign up options and extra legal text. I selected three thanks to feedback received in Design Review, then prepared prototypes for user test.

Test and Adjust

In user testing, I observed that one layout resulted in less errors than the others. However, no clear insights emerged around some of the tricky points in the journey, like signing up directly from the social media platform. This was likely because to gain time and reduce costs, I had to compromise in recruitment: participants were from the market, but based in the US.

Results

  • Time to join was reduced by 25s
  • Drop outs in the sign up form decreased by 10%
  • The first A/B tests around promoting sign up in the checkout resulted in increased sign ups. Some other ideas are still being tested.
  • The local sign up has not launched yet.

After 4 months in the team, I had to join another team (due to cut hours with the Covid-19 situation). When I left, they thanked me for helping them to become more user-centered, more data-driven, and more confident in prioritising their backlog and handling stakeholders.
