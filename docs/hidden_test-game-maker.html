Testing a game maker with children

BBC - 2015/2016

The challenge

Test the BBC's various iterations of a games and stories maker to ensure it is:

  • easy to get into for first time players,
  • fun and rewarding for fast-learners and advanced players,
  • accessible to children with poor vision, attention deficit or dyslexia.

What I did

For each round of testing, I worked with another UX Consultant. We alternated the roles of lead researcher and supporting researcher.

Iterative testing

Some of the rounds of research were conducted in a lab. For others, we met children at their home during school holidays. This meant we were able to gauge how the experience was affected by real-life situations (like being interrupted by siblings). As a result, we gained a better view of why children got frustrated and lost interest in the game. One example was that the distinction between the "Play" and "Edit" mode wasn't clear enough so kids forgot which mode they were in.

Typically, for each session:

  • I started by introducing myself and chatting with the children about the games they liked in order to put them at ease.
  • Then, I let them freely explore the game for a while to see what they made of it and what they naturally managed to do.
  • Next, I asked them to complete some tasks to cover specific features that the product team was anxious to get tested.

Results

  • The first version of the game was frustrating for children, who often got stuck. Testing allowed to find out what was wrong and fix it. For instance, the new versions of the games introduce complex concepts like object physics more carefully, so no prior knowledge is needed to understand how it will affect objects.
  • Testing also allowed to refine the themes and visuals. The initial game was described as 'childish' by kids, who wouldn't have wanted their friends to know they played it. The most recent games are so cool that kids aren't afraid of sharing their creations on social media.
