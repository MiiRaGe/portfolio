Re-organising the content of the product page

Thomas Cook - 2017

The challenge

Make it easier for users to find what they are looking for on the product pages of the Thomas Cook website, which contain detailed information about the hotel, possible flights and board basis options.

What I did

I was part of a multi-disciplinary squad (Analytics, Content, Dev, PM, QA, UX) in charge of the re-design of the product page.

Review quantitative data

I worked with my colleague from the Analytics team to build a good picture of which pieces of information seemed to cause the most issues, based on data from the customer support teams and interactions recorded in Hotjar.

User testing

The quantitative data allowed to identify a number of issues, but in some cases further testing was needed to clarify why there were issues. So I conducted remote unmoderated user testing which showed that:

  • some content wasn't where users expected it to be.
  • several section names sounded similar, which made it difficult to know what content to expect in each section.
  • the structure was too deep: after expanding a few subsections without finding what they were looking for, users just gave up.

I presented the results to my colleagues in the squad, and we agreed that we needed to change the way the content of the page was organised.

Card Sorting

I organised an online card sorting exercise to find out how users naturally group the content and name the sections.

The results?

  • A couple of sections of the page weren't actually groups users naturally thought of.
  • Users chose section names that are more descriptive and shorter than the current names.

I presented these results to the squad. There were some surprises, but we still managed to agree on a new way to organise the content that we believed would work better.

Tree Testing

We needed to validate our proposition before changing thousands of product pages. To do that, I organised 2 tree tests: the first one for the proposed organisation, and the second one for the current organisation.

The results helped to validate the changes by showing that with the new tree, users can find the information that was difficult to find before, and it takes them less time to get there.

Results

We just finished the tree testing. Once the changes are implemented, all product pages will have:

  • Content that is organised logically, in sections that reflect what our users are looking for.
  • Section names which are short and describe exactly what each section contains, in our users' own words.
  • A structure which is more flat: only 2 levels of headings.
