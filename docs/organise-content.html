Thomas Cook, 2017

Improve the Hotel Details page

The challenge

Improve the Accommodation pages of the Thomas Cook website, which contain detailed information about the hotel, possible flights and board options.

The goal? Increase progression to the next step of the booking journey and decrease contacts to Customer Service.

My role

UX Researcher, working within a Product team in charge of the Accommodation page.

The process

Understand the problem

Review quantitative data

I worked closely with the Analyst in the team to review data from customer service, and interactions recorded in Hotjar.

User testing

I conducted remote unmoderated user testing on Usertesting.com to dig deeper into why some issues occurred. The main takeaways were that:

  • some buttons were hard to discover.
  • some content wasn't where users expected it to be.
  • several section names sounded similar, so it was hard to know what content to expect in each section.
  • the structure was too deep: after expanding a few subsections without finding what they were looking for, users just gave up.

Workshop

A workshop was led by the Product Owner and Design Lead. I presented an overview of all the issues encountered on the Accommodation page.

At the end of the workshop, we had a prioritised list of opportunities to act on. High up the list was re-organising the content on the page, and increasing affordance for key features.

Re-organise the content

Card Sorting

I organised a card sorting exercise on Optimal Workshop to find out how users naturally group the content and name the sections.

The results? A couple of sections of the page weren't groups users naturally thought of, and users chose section names that are more descriptive and shorter than the existing ones.

I presented these results to the team. There were surprises, but we managed to agree on a new structure to test. Here are some examples of changes:

  • A lot of content about activities and food existed as collapsed subsections under a "Your Hotel" section, which we removed.
  • Some sections got merged, like "More Flights Options" and "Your Flight Details" that became "Flights & Transfers", or "Just for Kids", "Sports and Relaxation" and "Everything else" that became "Activities".
  • Some sections got different names (e.g. "Swim & Sun" became "Pool & Beach", "What's included in the packages?" became "Packages", etc.)

Tree Testing

We needed to validate our proposition before changing thousands of product pages. The tasks in the test were set up using the most frequently asked questions to customer service. There wasn't much buy-in in sister companies in Germany, Belgium and the Netherlands: they felt some things would only work in English. So I got help from colleagues there to set up similar tests in German, French and Dutch.

The results? In most cases, increased success rate, less hesitation and lower completion times. But there were a few things to fine tune. For instance, a section about the location of the hotel was added (instead of having that information in the general blurb about the hotel). I organised a new test to validate this new proposition.

Re-design and validate

In some cases, design changes were needed in order to fit the new structure. This was for instance the case with the videos of hotels. In those cathat the designe rprototyped and I tested with users.

Results

With the new structure:

  • Content is organised in sections that reflect what users are looking for.
  • Section names are short and describe exactly what each section contains, in users' own words.
  • The structure is more flat: 2 levels of headings instead of 4.

Changes were gradually rolled out to measure effects. For instance:

  • Changes around Room Details and Board Type led to an increase of 3.5% in conversions.
  • The new location and design for videos led to an increase of 349% in users engaging with that content (and interacting with the video has the highest associated conversion rate of all clickable elements on the page).
